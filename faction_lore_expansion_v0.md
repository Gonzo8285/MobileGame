# Faction Lore Expansion v0 — The Curse of Gallowfell

_Authored 2026-05-22 by Controller (round 3). Extends — does not retcon — `faction_bible.md` v1, `lore_gallowfell.md` v0.1, and `archetypes_v0.md` v0.1. Each of the five factions gets a 500–700-word brief covering founding myth, central tension, three named Pillar characters, aesthetic motifs, three setpiece locations, what they want, what they fear, who they hate and why. Names of locations stay inside biomes already locked by `lore_gallowfell.md`. New Pillar characters do **not** duplicate Warlord names from `warlords_v1.md`; they are named NPCs the player meets in events, shrines, and chapter-arc beats._

**Status:** v0 expansion. Loreboard-feedable. No retcons.

---

## 1. Iron Penitents — Cathedral Ruins

**Founding myth.** Three centuries before Gallowfell was cursed, the village of Vornau-on-the-Hill (long since absorbed into the lower Cathedral district) buried twelve children in a single winter to the lung-fever. The local priest, Brother Aelric of the Open Hand, climbed the bell-tower on the twelfth night and held a hammer to his own skull until dawn — because, he said, _the children paid in suffering and we did not match the price._ Aelric was found alive but blind, his prayers half-coherent, his scalp split. He preached for forty more years on the doctrine that **suffering is the only currency the gods accept,** and that _what the world owes, the believer must pay in person, in blood, on the same stone the world bled on._ The Iron Penitents are his order. They believe the Monarchy's national gallows is the holiest site of suffering they know, because the dead there did the paying for everyone else.

**Central tension.** They believe their flagellation _frees_ the trapped souls of Gallowfell — but the bell-tower of their own Cathedral Ruin still rings on its own, and the Hierarchs privately wonder whether the curse is _answering them back_ or _eating them._ They keep marching anyway. The doctrinal split between the Hammer-Hierarchs (who believe pain is offered) and the Open-Vein Pilgrims (who believe pain is _given_, by force, to the lane) is real and unresolved. It is not a heresy yet, but only because no one in authority is willing to name it.

**Three Pillar characters.**

- **Hierarch Maccus of the Nail-Choir** — the eldest Hierarch, voice ruined by self-mortification, presides over the Long Aisle each dawn. Believes Penance-Captain Vyrrun is the prophesied "Captain of the Closing Aisle." Carries the **Hammer of the Open Hand** — a relic descended from Brother Aelric's original hammer, claimed (unverified) to have been the one held to his own skull. Maccus has never put it down. His arm is bound in a permanent splint.
- **Sister Vrendys, the Hooded Confessor** — Maccus's number-two and chief doctrinal critic. Believes the curse is feeding on Penitent suffering, not being undone by it. Has stopped flagellating in private. Still publicly leads the Procession every Hanging Day. Carries a single nail in her teeth at all times; she will not say why.
- **Brother Olfric, the Bell-Striker** — the order's youngest full Hierarch (nineteen). Climbs the Cathedral Ruin's bell-tower at the Hanging Hour each combat and tolls the bell himself — because, he insists, _the curse will not toll it for us forever, and if it stops, the lane has lost the offering._ Olfric is the order's morale glue; if Olfric ever stops climbing, the doctrinal split breaks open.

**Aesthetic motifs.** Rust-red robes over scarred bare backs. Iron helmets shaped like nails — faceless, no eye-slits, the wearer is meant to see only through prayer. Chains dragging from wrists, weighted at the ends with cathedral keys. Banners stitched from old prayer scrolls — the lettering still legible, the parchment yellowed. Hammers + chains + spilled wax. Bleeding crowns are tattooed on the chest of every full Hierarch (one drop per year of service). Palette: oxblood, dull iron, candle-yellow.

**Three setpiece locations (Cathedral Ruins biome).**

- **The Long Aisle.** A 200-metre nave still mostly intact, lit only by candle-stations every twelve paces. The pews have been split lengthwise and reformed into kneeling-rails — the Penitents kneel on the splinters as part of dawn rites. The chapter-2 boss arena is at the Aisle's altar end.
- **The Bell-Tower of Brother Olfric.** The Cathedral's east tower. Still standing, still ringing — the bell tolls on its own at the Hanging Hour and Brother Olfric joins it manually. Setpiece for a chapter-1 elite encounter and a fixed shrine.
- **The Vault of Open Hands.** The crypt below the Aisle. Holds the order's ossuary — every Hierarch since Brother Aelric is bricked into the wall, palms outward, the hand-bones varnished. Players unlock the Vault as the chapter-1 secret event _The Long Hand-Count_.

**What they want.** They want to be the order that ended the curse by suffering enough on its behalf. They want Aelric vindicated. They want the bell to stop ringing on its own — and to ring on their command, instead.

**What they fear.** That the curse is _consuming_ their suffering rather than being paid by it. That every nail driven in is feeding the wrong god. That the Hanging Hour belongs to something none of them can name.

**Who they hate and why.** They hate the **Ash-Mourners** — passionately, doctrinally, openly. The Mourners are the Monarchy's court, the same court that signed every death-warrant the gallows ever consumed. The Penitents see Mourners as _the cause_ of the suffering they're trying to undo. The Mourners see the Penitents as _useful idiots,_ which is worse.

## 2. Ash-Mourners — Catacombs

**Founding myth.** When the Monarchy's last plague-year (the Year of Cold Smoke, three generations ago) reduced the imperial capital by half, the court's official mourner-corps was overwhelmed. **Lord-Justiciar Vey the First** — ancestor of the current flagship-unit namesake — issued a single decree from the throne-room: _"We will not mourn one at a time."_ The court mourners abandoned their per-soul rites and developed instead the **Litany of the Cumulative Dead** — a single rite that mourned all the empire's dead, perpetually, as one ongoing procession. The litany was meant to be temporary. Three generations later it has never been suspended. The Ash-Mourners are the Litany's keepers. They are the Monarchy's last working organ. The court itself is hollow.

**Central tension.** They are funeral wardens who **draw power from the dead they are meant to mourn.** Without the death-essence of Gallowfell, the court ends. They cannot stop draining. They also cannot, in any honest theological sense, claim to be _mourners_ any more — they're parasites in court-wear. The senior Justiciars know this. They keep the Litany running anyway, because admitting otherwise dissolves the Monarchy.

**Three Pillar characters.**

- **Lord-Justiciar Vey, the Witness in Black** — the namesake of `faction_bible.md`'s flagship card. Current sitting Justiciar. Has not slept in eleven years, by reputation. Signs death-warrants in his own blood. Believes the Court can be redeemed by burying Gallowfell properly _at last_. Speaks softly. Wears the gilt-bone mask of his great-grandfather, which is loose on the face and clicks faintly when he breathes.
- **Court-Mortifier Eulia Mae, Keeper of the Long Dirge** — Vey's chief ritualist and the order's senior choir-mistress. She conducts the perpetual Litany from a stone podium in the Catacombs' central chamber; the choir does not pause for sleep — singers rotate in eight-hour shifts. Eulia herself has not left the podium in three years. She is the only person in the order who has openly named the parasitism. She still conducts.
- **Page-of-Warrants Solenne, the Ink-Stained** — youngest of the order, an orphan-novice with ink-permanent fingertips and a courier's stamina. She carries Vey's warrants between the Catacombs and the Gallows Hill nightly. The Saint That Should Not Hang reveals her name to Solenne first, mid-Chapter-3, in a locked event.

**Aesthetic motifs.** Black brocade court-wear gone half-rotten. Gilt-bone masks. Signet rings on skeletal fingers. Ravens on every shoulder. Ink-stained gloves. Crowns + warrants. Black writs. Sealed bone. Palette: bruise-purple, tarnished gold, grave-grey.

**Three setpiece locations (Catacombs biome).**

- **The Hall of Standing Dead.** A 400-metre corridor of mass burials, the dead positioned upright against the walls, each sealed in place with a single court signet pressed into wax over the heart. The Hall is where the Chapter-1 boss (The Black-Bell Choir) is encountered.
- **The Litany Podium.** Court-Mortifier Eulia's permanent station. Centre of the order's authority. A REST node and a fixed Coven shrine site. The podium is built from a single block of ash-grey marble carved with the names of every dead Justiciar.
- **The Warrant Vault.** Holds three centuries of signed death-warrants. Player accesses it during a chapter-3 secret event _Read Your Own Name_ — the warrant for the player-Warlord's own future execution is, canonically, already on file. The player chooses whether to burn it or seal it.

**What they want.** To preserve the Monarchy. To bury Gallowfell properly. Privately, individually, in whispers — to stop draining the dead.

**What they fear.** That the Litany of the Cumulative Dead has _become_ the curse — that they are not draining death, they are _feeding_ it. That the Saint That Should Not Hang will name them, individually, by warrant. That the choir will fall silent one shift and no one will start it again.

**Who they hate and why.** Privately, the **Iron Penitents** — for the disrespect of self-flagellation in a place of state-mourning. Publicly, _nobody_ — the Court does not stoop to faction-hatred. The performance is the point.

## 3. Coven of the Black Mire — Bog of Bargains

**Founding myth.** The Black Mire is older than the empire. Older than the Cathedral. The Bog-folk who live in it have always lived in it. They were not _founded_ — they made a bargain. A thousand years ago a hoofed thing crossed the bog at dawn and left thirteen hoofprints in the mud. The thirteen women who saw it bargained: their thirteenth daughters in exchange for the Mire's _names_. The Mire knew the names of every drowned thing in its water. The bargain held for thirteen generations. Then the gallows-water — the runoff from the Cathedral's executions, draining south down the slope into the Mire — began washing the names clean. The Coven's gods have gone quiet for the first time in a thousand years. The hoofprint is still in the mud. The patron has come back to walk Gallowfell. The Coven follows.

**Central tension.** They are a debt-collecting people whose debt is no longer being paid. The bargain that defined them is breaking. They believe the gallows-water has stolen the Mire's names; they want them back. **But their patron demon walks Gallowfell now, openly, and they cannot tell whether they are following a god or being hunted by a creditor.** Marsh-Mother Eddra refuses to clarify which.

**Three Pillar characters.**

- **The Thirteenth Thirteenth** — current matriarch of the founding line. (She is the thirteenth daughter of the thirteenth bargain.) Cannot speak above a whisper; her throat was the price of the most recent re-negotiation. Counts cards by laying them out as fortunes. Believes the patron is hunting them. Has buried six of her thirteen children herself, by her own hand, per a bargain none of the order will discuss.
- **Old Faun, the Briarwright** — the order's craftsman. Weaves the leech-belts and demon-coin pendants. Lost both eyes to a swamp-imp at age eight; reads cards by touch. Has shod the entire Coven's children in stilt-boots since the gallows-water rose. Believes the Mire wants its names back, and that the patron is a distraction. Quietly dissident.
- **The Coin-Spinner** — a nameless child (estimated age nine, though the Coven won't say). Found floating face-up in the bog wearing one of the demon-coin pendants no one in the Coven made. Will not speak. Spins coins on the back of one hand all day, every day. When the Coven runs critical decisions past her, she stops spinning the coin and looks up at one specific person — and that person dies before the next dawn. The Coven asks her permission for nothing.

**Aesthetic motifs.** Bog-mud skin under tattoos. Bone-white teeth. Leech-belts. Demon-coin pendants. Third eyes painted on. Familiars with one too many limbs. Coin + briar. Cloven hoofprint in mud. Bound dolls. Palette: bog-green, demon-coin gold, leech-purple.

**Three setpiece locations (Bog of Bargains biome).**

- **The Hoofprint Stand.** A single round of cleared ground where the patron's hoofprint sits in unaging mud. The Coven's central ritual site. Players access it as a chapter-2 fixed event _Step or Step Around_.
- **The Coin Pool.** A black-water pool 60 paces across where the demon-coins surface, face-up, with names on them. The names are sometimes those of player-Warlords. A chapter-3 secret event surfaces a coin with the player's own name; the player can recover it or leave it floating.
- **The Drowned Doll-Field.** A shore where bound-dolls accumulate, washed up by the gallows-water. Each doll is a "name the Mire forgot." Setpiece for the chapter-2 boss arena.

**What they want.** The Mire's names back. The patron explained. The gallows-water dammed. Their gods to speak again.

**What they fear.** That the patron is not their god any more, but a creditor come to collect. That the Coin-Spinner is not their patron's herald, but their patron. That every bog-bargain the founders signed is being audited at once.

**Who they hate and why.** The **Last Legion** — the Mire's water-supply runs through the Foundry's runoff channels, and the Legion has dammed and re-routed the gallows-water repeatedly to keep their forges fed. Every time the Legion engineers move a channel, a name washes clean.

## 4. The Last Legion — The Foundry

**Founding myth.** The Legion is what is left of an imperial regiment ordered to **hold Gallowfell against the dead** six years ago, by an emperor who has since died, by sealed orders that have never been rescinded. They marched in fourteen-thousand strong. They are now, by their own count, one-thousand-four-hundred and twelve. They have buried their generals (every officer above captain is dead, in the field, by either combat or curse-attrition) and have re-organised entirely around captains and the foundries — the black-coal forges of the Foundry district, which the Legion has run continuously since arriving. The Legion has not received an order in six years. They are still holding.

**Central tension.** Their orders were not rescinded. They cannot stand down without permission, and there is no one to give it. They are a regiment that has _outlived its empire_, still in formation, still in uniform, still showing up at dawn. They have begun to wonder whether the empire that signed their orders ever really existed — or whether _they signed their own orders, ages ago, and forgot._ The senior captains will not let the question be asked out loud. **They keep the forges stoked because the routine is the meaning.** The smoke from the forges has the same iron taste as their blood now.

**Three Pillar characters.**

- **Captain Halgrave the Younger, the Iron-Yard Master** — current senior captain, eight years in the field. Inherited command from his father (also Halgrave) who died at the third Hanging Hour of the second year. Halgrave the Younger never knew an emperor; he knows only the orders his father carried. Carries the regiment's last imperial standard, the cloth long since rotted, the iron pole intact. Believes _absolutely_ that the orders are still valid. Sleeps four hours a night.
- **Anvil-Captain Vrenn the Soot-Faced** — Halgrave's number-two and the order's chief logistician. Runs the forges. Has the regiment's payroll-book that has not been signed by an emperor in six years. Personally pays the soldiers, in iron tokens, from the regiment's strongbox. Believes Halgrave is correct that the orders stand; believes privately the forges are _what is keeping the regiment alive_, not the orders. Has begun designing weapons specifically to kill curse-risen enemies — a quiet doctrinal innovation.
- **Standard-Bearer Mira the Long-Walker** — youngest captain (twenty-one) and the regiment's morale officer. Walks the entire defensive perimeter of Gallows-side at dawn each day, four kilometres, to keep the standard visible. Has refused promotion three times. Believes the regiment is the only thing standing between Gallowfell and the empire's collapse. Has not been south of the wall since she was sixteen.

**Aesthetic motifs.** Blackened steel cuirasses with brass plate-rivets. Soot-painted faces. Factory aprons over uniform. Standards in iron, not cloth. The **Crowned Anvil** sigil. No officer above captain. Palette: soot-black, brass, ember-orange.

**Three setpiece locations (Foundry biome).**

- **The Iron-Yard.** Captain Halgrave's command post. Forge-yard, parade-ground, mess-hall, and command-tent compressed into one. Chapter-4 boss arena (Iron Communion). The yard's anvils are struck every dawn at reveille — the rhythm is unbroken since arrival.
- **The Payroll Strongbox.** Anvil-Captain Vrenn's iron-token vault. Holds three centuries of imperial token-currency the regiment has never spent. Fixed shrine for a chapter-4 economic-trade event.
- **The Long Perimeter.** Standard-Bearer Mira's daily walk — four kilometres from the south wall around the Foundry's outer rail-line back to the Iron-Yard. The Long Perimeter is a chapter-side-event chain ("Walk With Mira"); players join Mira for one of her dawn walks and learn the regiment's history through her commentary.

**What they want.** New orders. Or, failing that, confirmation that the old orders still stand. A standing-down day. A captain promoted to colonel. _Anything_ to confirm there is still an empire that knows they're here.

**What they fear.** That the orders were never valid. That the empire is gone and they are alone. That the forges will go cold one morning and they will not know how to relight them, because relighting was always done by someone whose name they have forgotten.

**Who they hate and why.** The **Skinward Pact**. The Skinward burnt the Cinderwood _before_ the Legion arrived, and the resulting smoke shortened visibility for the Legion's initial defensive lines. Six years on, the Legion still measures the Skinward Pact as an _operational hazard_, not a faction. The Skinward, conversely, regard the Legion as squatters.

## 5. Skinward Pact — Cinderwood

**Founding myth.** The Skinward worship the **god-tree** — a specific, named, mapped tree that grew in the centre of the Cinderwood forest north of the wall. The god-tree is the one the gallows-tree on Gallows Hill was carved from. When the empire's wood-cutters came north for timber four generations ago, the Skinward elders cursed every axe and the cutters left — except for one stand of the god-tree's lower branches that was felled and dragged south to become the founding scaffold of the gallows. The Skinward have remembered this for four generations. They want the wood back. They want the bones (the executed dead are, by Skinward theology, _part of_ the god-tree now, having been hanged on it). And they want a generations-late vengeance. Some of their elders insist the felled gallows-tree is _still listening_. None of them want to find out what it has heard.

**Central tension.** They are a people of patient grievance. Their grievance has now arrived: the curse has cracked the empire's grip on Gallowfell, and the way is open. **But the elders disagree, profoundly, about whether the gallows-tree wants vengeance or wants peace.** Some Skinward believe the tree is _suffering_ and the only mercy is to chop it down at last. Others believe the tree is _hungry_ and must be fed every executed dead it can swallow. The split is not yet open. Tree-Walker Mhar has refused to take a side. He carries the tree's last seed in his ribcage; he says the tree will tell him itself, when it's ready.

**Three Pillar characters.**

- **Elder Brenhilde of the Twelve Antlers** — the order's senior matriarch. Wears a crown of branching antlers (twelve points, accumulated one per decade of service). Believes the gallows-tree wants peace and the executed dead should be unburied and returned to the Cinderwood for proper composting back into root. Patient, soft-spoken, terrifying. Has personally adopted seven feral children. Carries a single bone-flute carved from her own first child's femur (the child died in infancy; Brenhilde has played the flute every dawn since).
- **The Two-Pelted Warden** — the order's mid-rank field commander. Wears two pelts at all times — one wolf (his familiar's), one his own grandfather's tanned skin. Believes the gallows-tree wants _vengeance_, that the empire's executed dead are owed back as kin, and that the Skinward must take Gallowfell by force before the curse swallows it. Open dissident from Brenhilde. Has not been seen in the same room as Tree-Walker Mhar in three years.
- **Cinder-Mute Hjar** — the youngest full-rite Skinward (eighteen). Lost his voice to a Cinderwood blight at twelve. Communicates only through hand-sign and through the crow-familiars he keeps. Believes neither Brenhilde nor the Two-Pelted Warden. Believes the tree is _dead_ and the Skinward must build a new theology entirely. Carries no weapon. The order tolerates him because he is, by all accounts, the kindest person in it.

**Aesthetic motifs.** Layered hide-cloaks. Antler-crowns sprouting fresh leaves through bone. Mismatched eyes. Smoke trailing from fingers. Bone-flutes. Pelts of the dead. Crow-familiars. Palette: ember-red, ash-green, bone-white, smoke-grey.

**Three setpiece locations (Cinderwood biome).**

- **The Stump of the God-Tree.** Where the gallows-tree was felled four generations ago. Still smoking. Still rotting. Still listening. Chapter-5 boss arena (Cinderwood Warden) is fought in its shadow.
- **The Antler-Crown Clearing.** Where Elder Brenhilde holds court. A circle of twelve standing-stones, each topped with a real antler given by a dead Skinward Elder. Players access a chapter-5 dialogue event with Brenhilde here.
- **The Burnt Hall of Cinder-Mute Hjar.** A half-burnt longhouse on the edge of the Cinderwood, where Hjar lives alone with twenty crow-familiars. A secret event: bring Hjar three names from the warrant-vault and he gives you a Skinward-Pact alt-art unlock.

**What they want.** The god-tree returned. The executed dead returned to the Cinderwood. The Cinderwood un-burnt. The empire to pay, but only in the specific currency of admitting the founding theft.

**What they fear.** That the tree is dead, not sleeping. That the bone-flutes will run out of bone. That the curse will swallow the Cinderwood as it has swallowed the Foundry. That none of the elders are right and the Skinward have no theology left at all.

**Who they hate and why.** The **Last Legion** (mutual — see §4). The Skinward see the Legion as the empire's last claim on _their_ stolen wood. The Legion sees the Skinward as fire-starters in the Foundry's blind spots. They have not formally engaged each other in six years, but each one has lost soldiers in skirmishes along the wall.
